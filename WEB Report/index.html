Why This Matters

Mental well-being among workers is a growing concern for policymakers across industrialized economies. South Korea, characterized by long working hours and hierarchical workplace cultures, presents an especially relevant case for studying the link between job quality and psychological health.

This research investigates whether composite job quality — measured through workplace safety, task autonomy, and management quality — is systematically associated with mental well-being as captured by the WHO-5 Well-being Index.

We employ a progressive control strategy: if the association survives the addition of demographic, work-time, and compensation confounders, it is unlikely to be spurious.

Data and Variables

The 7th Korea Working Conditions Survey (KWCS, 2023) covers a nationally representative sample of the Korean working population. We restrict our analytical sample to wage workers (n = 26,203) to ensure comparability in employment conditions.

All index variables are scaled to 0-100 via min-max normalization to allow direct coefficient comparison across dimensions.

Coefficient Stability Across Models

The Job Quality coefficient drops from 0.166 to 0.050 as controls are added — ~70% explained by demographics, hours, and compensation. The residual 30% remains highly significant.

Conclusions

Key Findings

1. Robust Association

The Job Quality coefficient survives all four control specifications (0.166 to 0.050), indicating the relationship is not driven by demographic composition, working hours, or compensation.

2. Management Dominates

Supervisor respect, coordination, and support account for the largest share of the well-being association. The management coefficient (0.222) is 5x the effect of physical safety and operates in the opposite direction from autonomy.

3. Autonomy Paradox

Task autonomy shows near-zero (slightly negative) effect once management is controlled, suggesting collectivist workplace norms in Korea may moderate its value relative to Western contexts.

Limitations

Cross-sectional design precludes causal inference. Reverse causality is plausible: happier workers may rate supervisors more favorably. Self-reported measures introduce common method variance.

Future work should exploit longitudinal KWCS panels or natural experiments (manager rotations, plant closures) for identification. Instrumental variable approaches using organizational restructuring could address endogeneity.

Policy Implication

Supervisor training and management practice interventions may yield greater mental health returns than structural task redesign alone. Occupational health policy should elevate psychosocial factors — particularly leadership quality — alongside traditional hazard reduction.

A one-standard-deviation improvement in management quality is associated with a ~4.4-point gain in WHO-5, a clinically meaningful shift equivalent to moving from "low well-being" to the population mean.
